Instruction

As an instructor, my top priority is empowering learners to create ethical, meaningful, and innovative digital composition. I do this by structuring my courses to include many stages of drafting and revision, offering extensive, formative feedback, focusing on hands-on creation and problem solving, and setting high standards and the means and resources to achieve success.

Agile in the Classroom

Agile Purpose

After teaching students in the School of Media Arts and Design for a decade, I realized to truly prepare students for an evolving workforce that demands creative problem-solving and adaptability, I needed to integrate Agile Methodology into my course. In preparation for the course, I became a Certified ScrumMaster then partnered with an Agile Facilitator from an educational tech company to develop a course that used the Agile framework to create a class-wide WordPress site. I wove Agile principles and exercises throughout the curriculum and gave the students experiential learning implementing Scrum. My priority as an educator and a leader has always been to foster self-organization and creative problem solving, and no previous course had demonstrated such student engagement and growth.

The Vision

An interdisciplinary course built on Agile Methodology so students can gain hands-on experience with Scrum while creating a professional website that will serve the university.

The Impact

The course was truly transformational. Students grew into their roles through mentorship, retrospectives, and understanding that the problems they encountered were theirs to solve as they saw fit.

Agile Impact

"It allows students the ability to really work in self-organizing teams to help reach maximum efficiency that we can't do in other classes. Agile increases trust between team members and helps create meaningful products quickly instead of wasting time. I think introducing the Agile method in more classes would allow for increased productivity and foster student's leadership skills. The Agile method is being utilized by many organizations, and exposing students to it early in their career could set them ahead." — Communication Studies Major
Tutor Training

Consultant Training Purpose

In this course, I trained students to become effective tutors of digital communication. I recruited and vetted students from Communication Studies (SCOM), Writing, Rhetoric, and Technical Communication (WRTC), and The School of Media Arts & Design (SMAD), because each of their disciplinary perspectives contribute to successful and compelling digital communication. This class is a locus for these majors to share and cultivate that expertise in order to better serve the university.

The class frames that expertise with the training of the best strategies for college tutors, via theory, research, and practice. Tutors for the Learning Centers develop vital professional skills: pedagogical aptitude, professional interpersonal communication, and experience with cross-disciplinary perspective on digital texts.

Training Pedagogy

The tutor training course blazed new ground within Peer Instruction, so I pulled from many different sources to develop the curriculum: I incorporated Peer Tutoring pedagogy, Writing Center practice, and texts on designing effective digital portfolios in addition to extensive hands on production with a range of web builders, writing documentation and resources, and a series of Apprenticeship exercises and reflection essays as the students develop both their digital creation and tutoring skills.

I focused on training the students for two very different roles:

  • As peer tutors for one-on-one consultations for students working on digital assignments
  • As course-embedded tutors who would partner with faculty and give presentations and workshops on the course's digital assignment

The first course culminated with a presentation to stakeholders for the program, which gave the students the opportunity to craft the final version of the program we wanted to launch. Their voice in shaping the parameters of the program were invaluable through our pilot year; I saw time and time again the tutors' investment and commitment to a program they understood to be their creation.

Pedagogy

One-on-One Informed Pedagogy

Behind the Scenes of an Assignment

My journey as an educator has always been complimented by my work as a consultant: first at a Writing Center, a Communication Center, and then running DigiComm. In every situation, I saw the "behind the scenes" issues students face with assignments. This has always informed my instruction and shapes the ways I approach assignment design, so I can inform my learners' process as much as their end product.

When I collaborate with faculty across disciplines, I can inform their curriculum, pacing, and resources to best serve their students to create effective writing, presentations, and digital content.

Process-focused Pedagogy

Multimodal and digital composition require many stages: drafting, trouble shooting, collecting data and content, building layouts, crafting narrative flow and purpose, revising, and usability testing.

From my vantage point with one-on-one consultations, I have had a unique opportunity to observe students as they work through the complex and often circular process of digital composition. I've helped both faculty and students to have more honest conversations about the importance of mistakes and iteration, and ultimately how that informs our expectations of the composition process and the creation of more sophisticated projects.

As a content creator and learner, I am attuned to tutors' and students' perspective and knowledge of digital media and can capitalize on those insights, as well as redirect students to intrinsic learning objectives and the value of digital communication outside of the classroom dynamic.
